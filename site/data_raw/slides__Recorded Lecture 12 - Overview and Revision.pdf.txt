---PAGE_BREAK---

Avoid brute force
Try to avoid brute-force solutions.
Exhaustive search that tries each possibility (one at a time) in order
to determine if it is the solution.
▶Very often brute-force solutions will lead to algorithms with exponential
complexity.
▶Sometimes brute-force solutions still yield polynomial algorithms. But even
in those cases, it is often possible to solve the problem more efficiently
using other approaches.

---PAGE_BREAK---

Reduction/adaptation
Is the problem you are trying to solve a variant, extension,
special case, . . . of another problem for which you already
know an efficient algorithm?
Can you use that efficient algorithm as a subroutine to solve the new
problem? A slightly adaption of that algorithm?
If the new problem is a special case of the more general problem,
can you improve the efficiency of the general algorithm for solving
this special case?

---PAGE_BREAK---

Divide-and-Conquer approach
Is there an efficient way to divide the problem into smaller
sub-instances, solve them and recombine the solutions of the
sub-instances to get the solution of the original problem?
Can be useful when you already know a polynomial time solution but
are trying to improve the efficiency further.
Try to divide the work as equally as possible. Remember the
difference in worst-case performance between Merge Sort (which
always achieves a good split) and Quicksort with bad pivots.
8/24

---PAGE_BREAK---

Greedy algorithms
Make a locally optimal choice at each stage and commit to it.
It is often easy to come up with ideas of greedy algorithms to solve a
problem, but many times they do not produce optimal solutions.
Correctness proofs are very important.
Try your greedy ideas on small inputs. If it fails, it will often give
you further insights into the problem you are trying to solve. If it
works, try to develop a correctness proof for your idea.
9/24

---PAGE_BREAK---

Greedy algorithms
Correctness proofs of greedy algorithms using “exchange
arguments”:
▶Consider an optimal solution and the solution output by your algorithm.
▶If they are equal, you are done.
▶Otherwise, keep changing the optimal solution step-by-step so that it
becomes more similar to the solution output by your algorithm. Prove that
no exchange step makes the solution worse.
10/24

---PAGE_BREAK---

Greedy algorithms
Correctness proofs of greedy algorithms by proving that your
solution “stays ahead”:
▶Prove that your solution is optimal by showing that at each step your
solution is ahead or at least equal to every other possible solution (in the
relevant metric to your problem).
11/24

---PAGE_BREAK---

Greedy algorithms
Many times you can come up with multiple different greedy ideas,
and none of them will work.
Sometimes you can come up with multiple different greedy ideas,
and only one of them works.
A few times there are multiple different greedy ideas that work (e.g.,
minimum spanning trees).
Sometimes greedy is a good heuristic to get a “good” solution to a
(combinatorial) problem even if not optimal (and thus used to get
“good” solutions to NP-complete problems).

---PAGE_BREAK---

Dynamic Programming
Do not repeat work, store and re-use!
Can you show that an optimal solution must be build from optimal
solutions to subproblems in a small number of possible ways?
Can you formulate the recurrence for that?
Very powerful algorithm design paradigm!
13/24

---PAGE_BREAK---

Network Flow
Can your problem be reduced to a max-flow problem? Min-cut
problem? Circulation with demands problem?
Very powerful tool for solving combinatorial problems!
And, of course, also problems about the flow of material/data.
14/24

---PAGE_BREAK---

Data structures
Certain data representations are more efficient than others for a
given problem.
Choose the most appropriate data structure for your algorithm as
this can impact its efficiency (e.g., priority queues for Dijkstra,
union-find for Kruskal, efficient search and retrieval data structures).
Principle of parsimony: choose the simplest data structure that
supports the operations needed by your algorithm (more supported
operations generally leads to less efficient data structures).

A small change to a problem. . .
Sometimes slight changes in the problem definition make new
techniques necessary to solve it.
For example, shortest paths on a graph:
▶For unweighted edges, it can be solved with an algorithm as simple as BFS.
▶For edges with non-negative weights only, it is possible to solve it using a
greedy algorithm (Dijkstra’s algorithm).
▶For edges with possibly negative weights, it is possible to solve it using
Dynamic Programming (Bellman-Ford algorithm).
18/24

---PAGE_BREAK---

Another small change to a problem. . .
Another example, unweighted vs weighted interval scheduling:
▶Unweighted interval scheduling (Problem 7 of Week 6 Applied): nrequests
with starting siand finishing fitimes. Goal: Find a subset of mutually
compatible requests of maximum size.
▶Greedy solution is possible: (1) sort requests by finishing time, (2)
sequentially process the sorted requests adding to the solution each request
that is compatible with all requests already in the solution.
▶Weighted interval scheduling: the requests also have weights wi. Goal:
Find a subset of mutually compatible requests of maximum total weight.
▶Weighted interval scheduling can be solved using Dynamic Programming.

---PAGE_BREAK---

Yet another small change to a problem. . .
Graph colouring:
▶Deciding whether an undirected graph is 2-colourable (Problem 1 of Week
5 Applied): It can be easily solved using BFS or DFS.
▶Deciding whether an undirected graph is 3-colourable?
▶NP-complete problem!

---PAGE_BREAK---

Small tweaks can complicate things a lot
Sometimes slight changes in the problem definition can make it
extremely hard to solve.
Another example, min-cut vs max-cut:
▶Ford-Fulkerson algorithm can be used to compute the min-cut in
polynomial time.
▶Computing the max-cut is a NP-hard problem.
21/24

---PAGE_BREAK---

Small tweaks can complicate things a lot
Yet another example, bipartite matching vs 3-dimensional matching:
▶Bipartite matching can be solved efficiently using network flow techniques
as we shown in the Seminar 9.
▶3-dimensional matching, the generalisation of bipartite matching in which
there are 3 sets X, Y, Z of nodes, and hyperedges that contain one element
from each of them is NP-hard.

What are the end goals?
Understanding algorithm design paradigms.
Developing a gut instinct of algorithmic complexity to be able to
make good decisions for real-world deployments.
▶Internalise the concepts of asymptotical complexity.

in your line of thoughts.
